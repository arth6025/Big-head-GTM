Most Shopify brands are invisible in AI search right now. Not because AI search is obscure — ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini are already driving purchase decisions — but because no one has built the content and signals that make AI engines trust and cite them.

This checklist fixes that. It covers the 25 actions that separate brands getting cited from brands getting ignored. Work through it in order. Be honest about what you haven't done. The score at the end tells you where you actually stand.

## Section 1: Audit & Baseline

Know where you stand before you do anything else. You can't close a gap you haven't measured.

- [ ] **1. Run an AI visibility audit on your store URL.**
  Why this matters: You need a baseline score — how often your brand appears across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude — before any other action makes sense. Without it, you're optimizing blind.

- [ ] **2. Manually ask 10 category-level prompts in ChatGPT and Perplexity and note whether your brand appears.**
  Why this matters: Your customers are asking questions like "best eco-friendly sunscreen under $30" and "most durable men's running shoes." If your brand isn't showing up in any answers, you have a visibility gap that's costing you sales today.

- [ ] **3. Identify your three closest direct competitors and check their AI citation rate.**
  Why this matters: AI visibility is a zero-sum game at the moment of recommendation. If your competitor gets cited and you don't, you lost the sale. Knowing their score sets your real benchmark.

- [ ] **4. List the 5 product categories or use cases your brand competes in and confirm whether you have any indexed content addressing each.**
  Why this matters: AI engines build answers from content that exists and is indexed. If you haven't published anything on "gifts for runners under $50" and your competitor has, the citation goes to them.

- [ ] **5. Check whether your brand is mentioned in any third-party listicles, review sites, or editorial content that AI engines commonly cite.**
  Why this matters: AI engines don't just pull from your site — they pull from the broader web. Third-party citations (Wirecutter, Reddit, niche review sites) are a major input. Knowing where you already appear shapes your outreach strategy.

## Section 2: Content Foundations

AI engines cite content they can trust, parse, and attribute. Most brand content isn't built for this. These six actions fix the foundation.

- [ ] **6. Create at least one FAQ page per major product category using exact shopper question phrasing.**
  Why this matters: Perplexity and ChatGPT heavily favor structured Q&A content when forming answers. A page titled "Is [Product] safe for sensitive skin?" written in natural question format is more citable than a product description page.

- [ ] **7. Publish a "best of" or comparison page for your category (e.g., "The Best [Product Type] for [Use Case] in 2026").**
  Why this matters: "Best X" is one of the most common prompt formats AI engines receive. If you publish a credible, well-structured version with real criteria and explanations, AI engines will pull from it when answering similar queries.

- [ ] **8. Write at least one authoritative long-form guide (1,500+ words) that answers a top-of-funnel question in your niche.**
  Why this matters: Long-form content with clear structure, citations, and specifics signals authority to AI engines. Thin content gets skipped. Dense, useful content gets cited.

- [ ] **9. Add structured data (schema markup) to your product and article pages.**
  Why this matters: Schema markup helps AI crawlers parse and attribute your content correctly. Without it, even good content can get orphaned or misattributed in AI-generated responses.

- [ ] **10. Ensure every piece of content includes a clear attribution signal — your brand name, product name, and a factual claim AI can repeat.**
  Why this matters: AI engines cite content they can paraphrase and attribute. If your content says "Brand X's Vitamin C serum contains 15% ascorbic acid, tested by a dermatologist," that's a citable fact. "Our product is great" is not.

- [ ] **11. Remove or consolidate thin, duplicate, or low-word-count pages that could dilute your site's authority signal.**
  Why this matters: AI engines evaluate domain-level trustworthiness. A site with 200 thin pages looks less credible than one with 40 well-developed pages. Pruning is as important as publishing.

## Section 3: Entity & Brand Signals

AI engines build a knowledge model of your brand. These five actions make sure that model is accurate, trustworthy, and reinforced across the web.

- [ ] **12. Claim and fully populate your Google Business Profile, even as an e-commerce brand.**
  Why this matters: Google's knowledge graph feeds into multiple AI systems, including Gemini. A complete, verified profile — with correct brand name, description, category, and website — strengthens your entity recognition across AI engines.

- [ ] **13. Publish a detailed "About" page that states your brand's founding story, mission, product categories, and key differentiators in plain text.**
  Why this matters: AI engines read About pages to build brand entity profiles. If your About page is three lines of marketing copy, AI has very little to work with. Give it facts: who you are, what you make, why it's different, who it's for.

- [ ] **14. Ensure your brand name, domain, and product names are consistent across your site, social profiles, and any third-party listings.**
  Why this matters: Inconsistent entity signals confuse AI knowledge graphs. If your Instagram says "BrandName Co." and your site says "BrandName," AI engines may treat them as different entities and fail to consolidate your authority.

- [ ] **15. Get your brand mentioned in at least two credible editorial outlets, niche blogs, or industry publications.**
  Why this matters: Third-party mentions from trusted sources are one of the strongest trust signals for AI engines. Press coverage, guest posts, and product features in niche media all count. Even one solid Wirecutter or Forbes mention can anchor your entity recognition.

- [ ] **16. Build a Wikipedia page for your brand (if you qualify) or ensure you appear in relevant Wikipedia articles.**
  Why this matters: Wikipedia is a primary reference source for AI knowledge graphs. Brands that appear in Wikipedia — even in a list of competitors or in a category article — are treated as verified entities. If you qualify, this is one of the highest-value actions on this list.

## Section 4: Prompt Targeting

Owning AI visibility means knowing exactly which prompts matter, then building content that answers them better than anyone else.

- [ ] **17. Research and list the top 20 AI search prompts your potential customers are using to find products in your category.**
  Why this matters: Prompt research is the AEO equivalent of keyword research. "Best moisturizer for dry skin over 40," "top protein powder without artificial sweeteners," "affordable running shoes for wide feet" — these are exact phrases your customers are typing into AI engines. You need the list before you can target it.

- [ ] **18. Map each target prompt to an existing page on your site or identify the gap where no page exists.**
  Why this matters: Every prompt should have a corresponding piece of content on your site. If 15 of your 20 target prompts have no matching page, you know exactly what to build next. Gaps in the map are revenue you're leaving on the table.

- [ ] **19. Prioritize your top 5 prompts by purchase intent and create a dedicated AI-optimized page for each.**
  Why this matters: Not all prompts are equal. "What is vitamin C?" is educational. "Best vitamin C serum under $50 for hyperpigmentation" is transactional. Start with high-intent prompts and build outward.

- [ ] **20. Use question-format headlines within your content (H2s and H3s) that mirror the exact phrasing of target prompts.**
  Why this matters: When AI engines scan a page to answer a query, they look for sections that directly address the question. A subheading that says "Is this serum safe for sensitive skin?" positioned before a clear answer dramatically increases the chance that section gets cited.

- [ ] **21. Monitor competitor content weekly and identify prompts where they rank but you don't.**
  Why this matters: Competitive prompt gaps are fast-win opportunities. If a competitor has a page targeting "best gym bag under $100 for women" and you don't, that's a citation you can take within weeks by publishing something better.

## Section 5: Tracking & Iteration

Visibility you can't measure is visibility you can't grow. These four actions give you a real feedback loop.

- [ ] **22. Set up a weekly tracker that manually or automatically checks your brand citation rate across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude for your top 10 target prompts.**
  Why this matters: AI visibility changes as engines update their models and as new content gets indexed. Without regular tracking, you won't know if your actions are working — or if a competitor just took citations you had.

- [ ] **23. Track inbound traffic from AI-referred sources in your analytics platform (look for referrals from perplexity.ai, chat.openai.com, and similar).**
  Why this matters: Citation without traffic is interesting but not actionable. Once you're getting cited, you should see referral traffic. If citations are rising but traffic isn't, something in your landing experience is breaking the funnel.

- [ ] **24. Review your AI-ready content once a month and update any page that contains outdated statistics, product information, or claims.**
  Why this matters: AI engines de-prioritize stale content. A page that was citable six months ago can drop out of responses if it hasn't been updated. Even small freshness signals — updated dates, new data points, revised statistics — keep content in rotation.

- [ ] **25. Run a quarterly full audit to re-score your visibility across all four AI engines and compare against the prior quarter.**
  Why this matters: Monthly tracking shows incremental movement. Quarterly audits show real trajectory. They also give you the data to make resourcing decisions — double down on what's working, cut what isn't, and identify new prompt categories before your competitors do.
